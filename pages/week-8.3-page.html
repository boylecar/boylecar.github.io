Week 8 - LED Audio Filter and Speaker

WHY?

Although I began college classes just last fall, I have been able to take some sophomore-level courses already and ENGR 203 is the last course in the "Electrical Fundamentals" series. By taking that class this summer, I will be on track as a Junior ECE student this fall. Because this class is the last in the fundamentals series, it makes sense that the lab project for this class is a term-long project that incorporates everything that we have learned so far in this series. For this reason, I decided that it was important to document this project more extensively here on my website to see how far I've come, and to see this notable landmark when I look back on my journey as an engineer. The assignment for this project is:

Design and build an audio filter light-show with a speaker module.

HOW?

The first step to completing this project was to choose a song that I would want to demonstrate for the lab demo. The song I chose is "Hold On, We're Going Home" by Drake, and using the app Audible I created a spectrogram of the song. A spectrogram is a graph with time on the x-axis and frequency on the y-axis, with magnitude of frequency indicated by a brighter color. Essentially, it shows what frequencies are most prominent in the song over time. Using this, I decided that everything under 750Hz should be filtered into the bass channel, everything above 3000Hz should be filtered into the high-tone channel, and everything in between would be the mid-range channel. After choosing the song and the frequency bands and creating a top-level diagram of the entire circuit, the next step was to design the filters and speaker module.

WHAT?

To design the filters for the low-range and high-range channels, I used second-order active filters with Sallen-Key topology. I designed these filters to be "butterworth" filters by using prototype capacitor values for values that would be easy to obtain, like a .1uF and a .05uF that you can make with two .1uF capacitors in series. Using these prototype values for the low-pass and high-pass filters and cutoff frequencies of 750 and 3000Hz respectively, I found the resistor values that I needed to build these filters. For the bandpass filter that would pass the mid-range tones, I used cascaded Sallen-Key filters where one was a lowpass filter with a cutoff frequency of 3000Hz, and a highpass filter with a cutoff frequency of 750Hz. These filters used an LM741 opamp, and for the speaker module which was a simple opamp circuit to amplify the audio signal in, I used an LM386 opamp. After testing this circuit, I realized that there wasn't enough voltage on the output of the filters to turn on an LED, but that the filters were working properly. To fix this, I designed another stage for each filter individually which would use an LMC6032 opamp to amplify the output and drive an LED. I also included potentiometers to adjust the speaker volume and individual channel light sensitivity, and finally a switch to turn of the speaker entirely.

REFLECTION

This project was exciting to work on and complete because of how much of my learning I was able to apply from my previous classes and how large the project was. There are certainly benefits to smaller projects that just test a specific area of knowledge or skill, but I personally find it more enjoyable to work on a larger project where there are many smaller problems to solve along the way. In this project, there was 5 distinct problems to solve that could've each been a small project on their own. The three filters, the speaker module, and the amplification stages were all distinctly different and are similar to lab projects I would've done last term in the Electrical Fundamentals series. To be able to put them all together into a kind of "portfolio" project like this was exciting and extremely rewarding. Going forward I think I would benefit more from spending less time on perfecting the initial desing, and try to actually build an initial design more quickly. There will always be real-world application problems that you can't forsee in your design and theory, so trying to get an initial design that will work and making improvements from there would've been better for this project.
