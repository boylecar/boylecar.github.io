Week 3 - Project Portfolio Website

WHY?

I believe that reflection is a critical aspect of both being a good engineer and living a good life in general. For nearly every project I create or discipline I pursue, I reflect on my actions and the results of the experience. Naturally, I asked myself how I was going to track my progress with my weekly projects and reflect on them when I finish each. Eventually, I had the idea to create a website from scratch where I could keep a record of my projects both for myself and as a project portfolio for potential employers to see what experience I have. So, my objective for this week was:

Create a website to track my planning, process, and reflection for each weekly project.

HOW?

Similar to my last project, this week's project would not benefit from an immense amount of planning, and was instead more focused on the process of solving small problems along the way toward my objective. However, it was very helpful to draw just a simple sketch on paper of how I wanted the website to appear before I even started coding it. Because I used flexbox for most of the styling of the website, a clear drawing ahead of time helped me visualize specifically if a flexbox would be horizontal or vertical and which ones would be contained in larger boxes. Along the way, I primarily used Google to quickly find solutions to problems I encountered or when I wasn't sure how to approach an issue. I used VSCode to edit the code for the website, and GitHub to store each iteration as I made major changes to the site along the way.

WHAT?

When I began this project, I substantially underestimated the time it would take to complete the outcome I was after, but slightly overestimated how difficult it would be. While it took many hours of solving small issues (mostly formatting with CSS), there was no single problem that was too difficult to be broken down simply and solved. As I mentioned, the most time-consuming and difficult part was trying to visualize how to divide each section of the website into flexbox parents and children. The best way I found to do this was to apply a border first to a parent flexbox to make sure it was the size I wanted. Next, I would decide what would be the child elements and put borders on them too, once again ensuring that the padding, spacing between elements, and flex-grow and flex-shrink properties were all correct. In addition to the low-level problem-solving required to achieve the desired appearance, it was also difficult to make high-level decisions about the website and how it should look. For example, in many ways it's easier to make all the boxes square and change their color or size, and much harder to decide if the website would look better if all the elements were square, all rounded, or perhaps a mix of both.

REFLECTION

Initially, when I was learning web development, I wasn't overly excited or interested. However, once I took a more active approach by creating a website of my own instead of only completing learning objectives assigned by the courses I was taking, web development became much more exciting. I enjoyed solving the small problems along the way and seeing the website slowly come together. Also, while I mentioned it was difficult to make high-level decisions about the appearance of the website as a whole, I learned to appreciate this aspect of the web development process when I get to challenge myself creatively. In this way, this project opened my eyes more than anything else to the fact that engineering is not only about the technical aspect of creation, but just as much about its purpose and how it's perceived or used by the intended audience or customer. I learned that as an engineer I will obviously have to solve technical problems, but I will also have to make decisions about appearance based on how to optimize the product for its purpose. In this case, my website had to be functional but also easy to read and navigate for potential employers.
