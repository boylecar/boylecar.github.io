Week 1 - Wooden Chest

WHY?

Since I received a Polaroid camera for Christmas when I was a kid, I have loved to take pictures that capture both spectacular and simple moments. Each photo captures a unique perspective that can never be copied or replicated. Until now I have kept my close to 100 polaroids in an old cardboard shoebox. I've always wanted to find a nicer place to store them, but never found a solution that excited me. Every option was either too expensive or not any better than my current one. Because of how important it is to me to store my polaroids in a place that is safe and looks good, I knew I had found the problem I would solve this week:

Create an effective storage solution for my polaroids that is visually appealing and protective.

HOW?

After defining my problem, the next step was to decide what criteria my design would have and how I would create it. I knew that it had to be fully enclosed with no light entering so that my polaroids wouldn't be affected. To make it look good and be strong while remaining generally cost effective, it was clear that some kind of box made from wood was my best option. Because I also had access to woodshop equipment, I settled on designing a wooden chest. The 3 major problems I had to solve were how to fit the sides together, how to attach the bottom and top, and what size it should be. By taking into account the appearance, strength, and ease of creating of different wood joints, I decided to use a rabbet joint for the sides and a tongue-and-groove joint for the bottom. The top, however, I would glue flat on the top of box and cut later so that the apparent "lid" of the box was really a little taller than just the thickness of the wood. Once the base and lid were assembled, I would sand both pieces and apply finish. Finally, I would attach the hinges and clasp with screws, making sure they were all centered. One unconventional decision I made to improve the design was to cross-cut two sides of the wood so the grain on each pair of sides was in a different direction.

WHAT?

In the process of actually building the wooden chest, the first step was to choose the right wood. I found a large piece of spare hardwood in my dad's shop that also looked great. To make both of the large sides of the board flat and parallel with each other, I put the board through the planer. After many passes and flipping the board each time, it was flat. To make the long edges square with faces, I put the board through the jointer. Now that I had a square and flat board, I began to make cuts with the miter saw to make smaller boards of the appropriate sizes. Next I made cuts using the table saw so that the sides of the box would fit together in a rabbet joint. I also cut those same pieces to create a groove where I would fit the bottom of the box. At this point, I used wood glue and clamps to secure the bottom piece to the sides and let it dry overnight. The next day I glued the top of the box flat onto the base, creating a hollow box. After it was done drying, I made a cut all the way around the box with the table saw at about 1 inch, separating the lid from the base. I used a chisel to remove the excess wood glue on the inside, and sanded inside and out with 80 grit sandpaper. Next I applied a clear gloss finish to both pieces, letting them dry by hanging them on fishing line and sanding lightly in between coats. Finally, I purchased two hinges and a clasp that I attached to the box with screws.
