Week 4 - Automatic Watch Winder

WHY?

When I was only about 10 years old, I was gifted a Mickey Mouse wristwatch for Christmas by my aunt and uncle. Both my passion for watches and my watch collection began at that time and would continue to grow gradually, even to this day. Now I own many different watches of all different kinds, sizes, and colors. All of my favorites, however, are automatic watches. This means that there's no electricity or battery powering the watch, and that its powered exclusively by mechanical winding of the spring, either by hand or through the natural motion of your wrist as you wear it. Naturally, if you don't wear an automatic watch for multiple days at a time, it will lose its power and eventually stop turning.

Create a system to wind mechanical watches automatically.

HOW?

Because I already have basic knowledge about how to use and control an Arduino (a programmable circuit board with microcontroller), I decided I would use this to control a stepper motor that would rotate the watch over time. First I had to purchase a kit for the stepper motor and learn how to program it to rotate how I wanted it to. Once I learned how to control the motor, I would have to create an apparatus to hold the watch itself, and a sturdy base to mount the motor to that keeps it still. The most cost effective way to achieve this for me is to use a 3D printer. For the base, I wanted to use some kind of wood that looked relatively nice and was heavy enough to counter the weight of the watch and winding mechanism. After the motor operates consistently and I create the base and watch holder, the only thing left is to assemble everything and hide the wires as much as possible to make the project look neat.

WHAT?

The first step for my project this week was purchasing a few stepper motors. As I waited for them to arrive in the mail, I wrote the code for my Arduino that would control the stepper motors. Once they did arrive, it was easy to plug one into the arduino to be activated. Additionally, I created a CAD model based on the schematics of the stepper motor that would create a tight fit for the stepper motor and the watch pillow too. However, the first model did not fit as desired. Even with a second itteration of a similar model, I still had not created a good enough piece to hold the watches and attach to the motor. Additionally, I had to abort the plan I had for machining a piece of aluminum to create a strong connection with the stepper motor because I didn't have access to a small enough drill bit. At the end of the week, I did not have the completed product I had envisioned, but I have still learned important lessons and skills through this week's project.

REFLECTION

Originally I had planned to complete the movement mechanism as well as the base on which I would mount the motor. However, I ran short on time due to several inadequate models of the watch holder and aluminum connector piece. I had the option to either finish my project in a rush or take the time to create something that was of a higher quality. I decided on the latter, and on not taking shortcuts in my work. I learned the valuable lesson that your project is not always going to follow the timeline you expect, and planning ahead by leaving room for mistakes is important. Also, I believe I made the right decision by not just "throwing together" an inadequate product just to meet my deadline. I think it's more important and more valuable to prioritize the quality of my product than taking shortcuts. I still feel satisfied with this project having gained the experience of using a 3D printer, spending more time using the milling machine, and programming an Arduino and designing/building functional circuits.
