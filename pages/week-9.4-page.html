WHY?

As we learn more and more about electronic components and the kinds of circuits that we can build in my Junior-level classes, I become more and more excited about the career that I chose. For our final project for my Junior Design class, we designed a voltage oscillator using opamps. This circuit is the most interesting that we have been able to design in this class, so for this reason I chose to include it as my project for this week.

Build a voltage oscillator using opamps

HOW?

From a high level, this circuit works by taking advantage of the fact that it takes tiem to charge and discharge a capacitor, as well as the ability to use opamps with feedback to have the output follow its non-inverting input. By connecting multiple opamps in series, each with their own RC circuit, you create a series of circuits that will have similiar wave forms on their output, but each be out of phase from one another.

WHAT?

At each of the opamps in this circuit with its output connected to the inverting input, the output of the opamp will follow the voltage at the non-inverting pin, due to the behavior of an opamp. When the voltage on the output rises, the voltage on the non-inverting input of the next opamp starts to rise determined by the rate set by the RC relationship. This is what causes the next wave to be slightly out of phase. This pattern continues, further shifiting the wave form and decreasing its magnitude from losses in the resistors, until finally the waveform reaches the primary opamp circuit, which has been set to a specific gain to account for the losses from the other opamps. Once the circuit is disturbed from its initial state, the outputs of the opamps will continue to oscillate indefinitely.
