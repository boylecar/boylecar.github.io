Week 6 - Automatic Watch Winder Pt. 3

WHY?

For the same reason as my first two weeks working on this project, I wanted to create and optimize an automatic watch winder that I can use in my everyday life to charge an automatic watch if it loses power. I have had a passion for watches ever since I was a young kid, and as my collection has grown over time I have began to face the problem of keeping them all running. This provided the opportunity for me to create a mechanism that would wind the watches with the force of gravity by rotating the watch and gradually winding the mainspring. Building off last week's design, my big-picture goal this week is to:

Optimize an automatic watch winder

HOW?

To add onto last week's iteration of my project, it is clear that the biggest improvement that should be made is in terms of the design's simplicity. Using an Arduino to control the motor was the only feasible solution I had access to in previous weeks, however it is not optimized for my project because the amount of unused components that make up the Arduino. Essentiallly the only components of the Arduino that I'm actually using is the microcontroller and the GPIO pins. The next logical improvement then is to design a system using only a microcontroller that replaces the Arduino. For this I would need to purchase a microcontroller which are very cheap, and a device to program it with. After researching I decided that a PIC microcontroller would be the best option for my project. I would need to learn some basic Assembly Language to create a program for the micrcontroller and use a compiler to actually tell the microcontroller what to do. I will simply attach the microcontroller to a breadboard and then to the pins for the motor to activate it.

WHAT?

The process of creating the 3D model of the part was simple and only took a few hours after I was given the dimensions that would accomodate the battery cells. I also modeled the metal plate that would secure the battery cells but have to wait for the materials to create it with. When I had the opportunity to learn how to use the milling machine, I spent several hours learning the fundamentals and practiced drilling holes and fitting each sides to the correct dimensions. The product of my tutorial is the aluminum block in the pictures to the left and above. After I understood how to use the machine, it was relatively quick to cut the plastic with a band saw, square and flatten the base with the milling machine, then make cuts to set the block to be the correct dimensions. Finally, I drilled the holes in the correct locations and at the right depth, so that I could attach the plates with screws later. The final step after drilling the holes was to tap them to the right size to create the threading for the screws. The most difficult parts of the entire process were learning how to use the machine and accomodating the tediousness of the tiny tolerances.

REFLECTION

My second project of this year, although much more simple than the first, was just as valuable in terms of how much I was able to take away about what it's like to be an engineer. I did a much better job with this project on planning and thinking through potential future problems before I actually began creating the physical model. While the planning process isn't quite as exciting, I continue to learn to appreciate this part of the process as necessary for producing a high-quality outcome. I also find significant enjoyment in the process of learning new skills, like 3D modeling with NX for this project and even more so the milling machine. This project made me realize that I enjoy the process of building/assembling the product and working with physical parts more than anything. This experience also helped grow my patience and attention to detail by requiring me to go very slow and be precise when making measurements that need to be accurate to within .0005 inches. Although this project was more skill-focused than it was outcome-focused, I am beyond satisfied with the lessons I learned and the outcome produced.
