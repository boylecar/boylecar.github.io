Week 2 - Battery Cell Testing Base

WHY?

The biggest reason I chose to study engineering was so that I could use my knowledge and problem-solving skills to contribute to society in a significant way and add value with my work. One potential path I am interested in pursuing is the production of electric cars. I think a future where electric cars have been maximized for performance and make our society more sustainable is a better, more exciting one. The Global Formula Racing(GFR) Club at Oregon State University works on this exact problem: building an electric car and maximizing its performance. Naturally, I got involved as much as I could contribute. The problem for this week that was assigned to me was:

Create a functioning base to test battery cell connections with different parameters.

HOW?

Although my project this week was certainly not easy, it was relatively simple and therefore didn't require much planning in advance. I spent roughly 5 hours per week my first term learning the basics of 3D modeling with NX, so I was already familiar with the process of creating the design. After that I needed to learn how to create technical drawings with the NX software. Next I would learn how to actually create a physical model of the base using a milling machine. Finally, I would create the actual physical design according to the dimensions of the technical drawing.

WHAT?

The process of creating the 3D model of the part was simple and only took a few hours after I was given the dimensions that would accomodate the battery cells. I also modeled the metal plate that would secure the battery cells but have to wait for the materials to create it with. When I had the opportunity to learn how to use the milling machine, I spent several hours learning the fundamentals and practiced drilling holes and fitting each sides to the correct dimensions. The product of my tutorial is the aluminum block in the pictures to the left and above. After I understood how to use the machine, it was relatively quick to cut the plastic with a band saw, square and flatten the base with the milling machine, then make cuts to set the block to be the correct dimensions. Finally, I drilled the holes in the correct locations and at the right depth, so that I could attach the plates with screws later. The final step after drilling the holes was to tap them to the right size to create the threading for the screws. The most difficult parts of the entire process were learning how to use the machine and accomodating the tediousness of the tiny tolerances.
