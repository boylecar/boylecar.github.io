Week 7 - Opamp Circuits

WHY?

I am very interested in pursuing a career related to electronics, circuit design, and PCB design, and so I decided that it would be good to expand on the circuits we are already building in my Junior Design class. Because we have already been talking about applications of amplifiers in my classes, my goal this week was pretty straightforward, to:

Design and build basic amplifier circuits and a precision rectifier.

HOW?

This week in our lab for Junior Design we were required to build a few different kinds of amplifiers, including a basic non-inverting amplifier with two supplies, an inverting amplifier with two supplies, and finally a single-supply inverting amplifier that is DC-biased to half of the supply voltage. Finally, I used the same opamp chip to build a precision rectifier with a high-frequency diode that stabilizes the output voltage and prevents any amplification of the input signal.

WHAT?

The circuit templates for these circuits were already given to us for the use in lab, so most of the work for this week's project was in choosing the component values, building the physical circuits, and testing/validation. Because of the direct feedback in these opamp circuits and the very high input impedance, it is possible to make amplifiers that have extremely precise gain, determined simply by a voltage divider that creates a specified voltage at the opamp input terminals. Using a 4.7k resistor and a 1.2k resistor to set the gain, I had a gain of about 4 for the inverting amplifier, (A = R2 / R1) and about 5 for the non-inverting amplifier (A = 1 + R2/R1). Finally, I was also able to build the precision rectifier so that it had an output that perfectly followed the input signal.

REFLECTION

It's always very rewarding for me to actually get to build the thing that I am designing, and so exciting to use the oscilloscope to visually see what happens to a signal at different points in the circuit. One concept that I find fascinating is the fact that feedback in a system is what allows us as engineers to make much more precise designs. When I was learning about how to build my 8-bit computer a long time ago, I remember hearing that "feedback is what allows for complex systems." In the context of that project, it wasn't until I added a conditional jump instruction (a form of feedback in computer architecture) that my computer could really do anything meaningful. A comparison was also made to that of the human mind, which is an extremely complex structure with very strong feedback. At a much simpler level, I am using feedback in these designs to extremely narrow the precision of these circuits. The importance of feedback across the board is something very interesting that I have began to notice more and more.
